# Worked Example — Customer Journey Map

A gold-standard run of this skill: a D2C subscription journey where the map's job is to prove the churn problem lives in month two, not at checkout — and to say plainly which parts are evidence and which are still guesses.

## The brief

Copperline Coffee — a fictional D2C specialty-coffee subscription with 48,000 active subscribers — churns 8.5% of subscribers per month, with median customer lifetime of 7 months. 62% of the last year's 1,120 cancellation-survey responses cite some version of "coffee was piling up." The retention team credits the current cancel flow (pause is offered only *inside* the cancellation path) with "saving" 19% of attempted cancels and is reluctant to change it. PM Iris Kalu has 14 post-purchase interviews, 12 months of cancellation surveys, 6 months of support tickets, and NPS verbatims (score 41, n=2,340). She's mapping the current state for one persona to decide where the next two quarters of retention work go.

## The output

# Customer Journey Map: Copperline Coffee Subscription

**Persona:** "Nadia, the supermarket upgrader"
**Journey scope:** Full end-to-end (awareness → advocacy)
**Current or future state:** Current state
**Prepared by:** Iris Kalu, Product
**Date:** 14 July 2026
**Based on:** 14 post-purchase interviews (Mar–Jun 2026), 1,120 cancellation surveys (12 mo), 6 mo support-ticket taxonomy, NPS verbatims (n=2,340). Awareness stage is **assumed** — no attribution research exists (see Research Gaps).

---

## Persona Summary

| | |
|---|---|
| **Name** | Nadia |
| **Role** | Marketing manager, 34, lives with one partner; 2 cups/day, ~1 bag every 2.5 weeks |
| **Goal** | Better daily coffee than supermarket beans without becoming a hobbyist |
| **Frustrations** | Decision fatigue in the specialty world; guilt about waste; hates feeling "managed" by retention tactics |
| **Tech comfort** | High for shopping, low interest in brew gear beyond a basic grinder |
| **Decision power** | Sole — but a $23/bag habit gets silently re-evaluated every time bags pile up |

---

## Journey Overview

```
AWARENESS → CONSIDERATION → DECISION → ONBOARDING → ADOPTION → ADVOCACY
```

**Overall experience rating (current state):** 😐 Neutral — a delightful first month wrapped around a month-two experience that quietly manufactures churn.

---

## Stage 1: Awareness

*How does the customer first discover the product exists?*

**Customer goal at this stage:** Realise supermarket beans are the reason home coffee disappoints.

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Trigger** | A markedly better cup somewhere else — a friend's kitchen, a specialty café *(assumed — no attribution data; flagged in Research Gaps)* |
| **Where they are** | Instagram, podcast ads, friends' kitchens |
| **What they do** | Screenshots the bag, searches the roaster, lands on comparison listicles |
| **Emotion** | 🙂 Curious — low stakes, mild aspiration |
| **Pain points** | Specialty vocabulary ("natural process," "washed") reads as gatekeeping; can't tell Copperline from five near-identical brands |
| **Opportunities** | Plain-language positioning ("better coffee, zero homework"); creator content aimed at upgraders, not connoisseurs |

## Stage 2: Consideration

*The customer is actively evaluating options. What do they do to decide?*

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Customer goal** | Pick one subscription she won't regret at $23/bag |
| **What they do** | Compares 2–3 brands; hunts for a discount code (74% of first orders carry one — analytics); reads reviews about *flexibility*, not flavour |
| **Touchpoints** | Website, review sites, Instagram comments, discount-code aggregators |
| **Emotion** | 😕 Wary — 5 of 14 interviewees mentioned a prior subscription "that was hard to cancel" as their reference fear |
| **Pain points** | Cadence options (weekly/fortnightly) don't match how she actually drinks; pause/skip policy is not findable pre-purchase |
| **Opportunities** | Publish the pause/skip policy on the pricing page; consumption-based cadence framing ("2 cups a day? That's a bag every 2–3 weeks") |

## Stage 3: Decision

*The customer is ready to buy — or not. What makes them commit?*

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Customer goal** | Commit with an exit clearly in view |
| **What they do** | Applies a code, picks fortnightly (the middle option, not a calculated one), completes checkout in ~4 minutes |
| **Touchpoints** | Checkout flow, cadence selector, confirmation email |
| **Emotion** | 🙂 Optimistic, slightly rushed — "I'll fix the settings later" (3 interviewees, near-verbatim) |
| **Pain points** | Cadence selector gives no guidance; defaults nudge faster cadence than her consumption; "fix it later" never happens |
| **Opportunities** | 3-question consumption quiz that sets cadence (cups/day, people in household, brew method); confirmation email restates how to change it |

## Stage 4: Onboarding

*The customer has bought. Now they need to get value fast.*

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Customer goal** | Confirm this was a good call — taste the difference |
| **What they do** | Unboxes bag one (roast-dated, tasting card), brews, upgrades nothing, tells someone about it |
| **Touchpoints** | Delivery/unboxing, tasting card, welcome email sequence |
| **Emotion** | 😊 Delighted — unboxing and first brew are the journey's peak; NPS 9–10 verbatims cluster in weeks 1–3 |
| **Pain points** | Welcome emails push gear and brew technique she didn't ask for; nothing in the sequence mentions skip/pause exists |
| **Opportunities** | Swap one gear email for a "your account, your pace" email (skip, pause, cadence) at day 10 — set up month two before it goes wrong |

**Key moment of truth:** The first cup that's visibly better than what she had before — it happens in week one, which is exactly why month two's problem is invisible to her until it isn't.

## Stage 5: Adoption

*The customer is using the product. Are they getting consistent value?*

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Customer goal** | Make it a background pleasure, not a logistics chore |
| **What they do** | Bag 3 arrives while bag 2 is half-full; she starts skipping mornings to "catch up"; opens the app to slow things down and finds no pause on the account page |
| **Touchpoints** | Deliveries, account page, support ("how do I pause?" is the #2 ticket category, 6-mo taxonomy) |
| **Emotion** | 😤 Guilty, then resentful — the product made her feel wasteful, then made the fix feel like a trap |
| **Pain points** | Cadence mismatch compounds silently; pause is only discoverable *inside the cancel flow*; 62% of cancellations cite surplus ("piling up"), peaking in months 2–3 |
| **Opportunities** | Pause/skip buttons on the account home; surplus detection ("skip next delivery?" nudge when cadence outpaces reorders); cadence-fit check-in email at day 30 |

**Adoption health indicators:**
- % of subscribers who adjust cadence or skip by day 45 (adjusters churn less — needs cohort confirmation, see Gaps)
- "How do I pause?" ticket rate per 1,000 subscribers
- Month-2→3 retention (currently the cliff: months 2–3 account for 47% of all churn)

## Stage 6: Advocacy

*The customer loves the product. How do you turn them into a referral engine?*

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Customer goal** | Share a genuine find without feeling like a salesperson |
| **What they do** | Serves it to guests, gifts a bag at holidays; rarely uses the referral link |
| **Touchpoints** | Referral programme, gift option, review-request emails |
| **Emotion** | 🙂 Quietly proud — but the referral offer ($15 off *for her*, nothing for the friend) "feels like being a coupon" (interviewee) |
| **Pain points** | One-sided referral reward reads self-serving; no low-effort gifting flow at the moment of serving guests |
| **Opportunities** | Two-sided referral (friend gets first bag discounted); "send a bag" one-off gift flow; review request timed to an NPS 9–10 response, not a fixed day |

## Emotion Curve

```
High  😊 │                              *
          │                                                      *
Neutral 😐│        *            *
          │  *
Low   😤 │                                        *    
          └────────────────────────────────────────────────────
            Aware   Consider  Decide  Onboard   Adopt   Advocate
```

**Lowest point:** Adoption (months 2–3) — surplus guilt plus a hidden pause control; 47% of churn happens here.
**Highest point:** Onboarding — first delivery and first cup; the brand cashes this goodwill in month one and spends none of it protecting month two.
**Biggest drop:** Onboarding → Adoption. The steepest sentiment fall in the journey, and the map's central finding: this is a cadence-and-control problem, not a coffee-quality problem.

---

## Prioritised Opportunities

| Opportunity | Stage | Impact on customer | Effort to fix | Priority |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pause/skip on account home (out of the cancel flow) | Adoption | High — removes the single most-cited churn driver; ends the "trap" feeling | Low | P1 |
| Consumption quiz sets cadence at signup | Decision | High — prevents the mismatch instead of managing it | Medium | P1 |
| Day-10 "your account, your pace" email | Onboarding | Medium — makes controls known before surplus starts | Low | P2 |
| Surplus-detection skip nudge | Adoption | Medium — catches mismatch the quiz missed | Medium | P2 |
| Two-sided referral + gift-a-bag flow | Advocacy | Medium — converts real advocacy the current programme suppresses | Medium | P3 |

**Noted tension:** the retention team's cancel-flow save rate (19%) will likely fall if pause moves to the account home — that 19% is partly customers who came to cancel because they couldn't find pause. The map's position: measure net month-3 retention, not saves. Escalated to the growth review, 28 July.

## What We Don't Know (Research Gaps)

| Gap | How to close it | Priority |
|---|---|---|
| What actually triggers awareness (Stage 1 is assumption) | Post-purchase attribution question + 5 JTBD interviews with new subscribers | Medium |
| Do cadence-adjusters actually retain better, or do healthy customers just adjust more? | Cohort analysis: day-45 adjusters vs matched non-adjusters on month-6 retention | High |
| How much of the 19% cancel-flow "save rate" is failed pause-seekers? | Add one question to the cancel flow + review session recordings (n=30) | High |

## Why it's shaped this way

- **One persona, and the awareness stage is labelled as assumption right in the header** — the quality checks demand one specific persona and that "the map reflects what is known, not assumed"; where evidence runs out, the map says so instead of writing plausible fiction.
- **Pain points are grounded in named data** — 62% of cancellation surveys, the #2 support-ticket category, near-verbatim interview quotes — per the anti-pattern "do not build the map from assumptions alone; ground at least the pain points in real customer data."
- **The pain points stay the customer's pain, even where they collide with a company win** — the 19% cancel-flow save rate is the retention team's success metric and Nadia's "trap"; the quality check ("customer's pain — not the company's pain") is what forces the map to side with Nadia and escalate the disagreement rather than average it away.
- **Stages are explicitly not weighted equally** — the map names Onboarding → Adoption as the biggest drop and puts 47% of churn there, per the anti-pattern against treating all stages as equal; Awareness and Advocacy get lighter treatment on purpose.
- **The emotion curve dips for real** — month two sits at 😤 even though onboarding is a peak, satisfying "the emotion curve shows the real experience — not an aspirationally positive version"; a curve that only rose would have hidden the entire finding.
- **Every opportunity is written as a buildable item and ranked** — "pause/skip on account home," "3-question consumption quiz" — per "specific enough to become backlog items" and the anti-pattern against unranked opportunities; P1s attack the biggest drop, not the easiest stage.
- **Touchpoints are Copperline-specific** (tasting card, cadence selector, cancel-flow pause) rather than generic SaaS furniture — the anti-pattern bans touchpoints "that apply to any product."
- **The research-gaps table protects the map's own conclusions** — the adjuster-retention correlation and the save-rate question are the two facts the P1 recommendations lean on hardest, so they're the two gaps rated High.
