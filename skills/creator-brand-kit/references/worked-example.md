# Worked Example — Creator Brand Kit

A gold-standard run of this skill: a rough two-paragraph brief from a woodworker with an inconsistent channel turned into a one-page kit sharp enough that every other content skill can write in her voice.

## The brief

Rhea Callahan spent 19 years as a commercial cabinetmaker before starting **Callahan Woodshop** — 42K YouTube subscribers, 6.1K on Instagram (as @rheabuildsstuff). Her uploads are a grab-bag: tool reviews (her best views, 60–120K), build vlogs, and occasional joinery deep-dives. She's launching a $149 course, *Joinery Foundations*, in October. The problem she can feel but hasn't named: tool reviews pull 3–4× the views, yet nearly all course-waitlist signups come from the joinery tutorials. She describes her audience as "hobbyists, I guess" and her vibe as "no-nonsense but not mean." She wants to grow, but mostly she wants the course launch to work.

## The output

```
# Brand Kit — Callahan Woodshop (Rhea Callahan)

## 1. Niche & positioning

- **Niche (specific):** Weekend woodworkers with garage-sized shops + furniture-grade
  joinery + hand-tool-first methods that don't require $10K of machinery. (draft — confirm)
- **Positioning line:** "I help weekend woodworkers build furniture they're proud to
  sign, using joinery that works in a one-car garage."
- **What makes you different:** 19 years on a commercial shop floor, translated to
  hobbyist constraints. Every other channel in the niche reviews tools or films
  timelapse builds; almost nobody teaches *why joints fail* from professional
  experience. You're not a gear channel — you're the person who's fixed a thousand
  callback repairs and knows which shortcuts actually hold.

## 2. Audience

- **Who:** 35–60, desk jobs, a garage or basement shop, $2–5K of tools accumulated
  over years. Skews evenings-and-weekends; a project takes them a month, not a video.
- **What they struggle with:** their finished pieces look "DIY" instead of
  "furniture" and they can't diagnose why — gaps in mitres, racking drawers, doors
  that bind by winter. Intimidated by the distance between YouTube builds and their
  own results. Suspect (correctly) that another $600 tool won't fix it.
- **What they aspire to:** an heirloom piece the family asks for. Signing their work
  without wincing.
- **Where they hang out:** YouTube long-form, woodworking forums/subreddits,
  regional woodworking shows. Instagram is a shop-photo habit, not a learning channel.

## 3. Content pillars

**Pillar 1 — Why Joints Fail (GROW)**
- Covers: failure autopsies — a joint, why it broke, the fix. Short, hooky, evergreen.
- Serves: the diagnosis pain above; it's the content nobody else has the shop-floor
  scars to make.
- Ideas: "This dovetail failed after 3 years. Here's the 2mm mistake." / "Why your
  mitres open up every January." / "Ranking 5 glue-ups from my repair bench: which
  joint actually failed?"

**Pillar 2 — Garage-Shop Builds (NURTURE)**
- Covers: full furniture builds under real constraints — one-car garage, no jointer,
  neighbour-friendly hours.
- Serves: proves the positioning ("furniture-grade in your shop, not mine") and gives
  the audience a project cadence they can actually follow.
- Ideas: "Hall table, hand-cut joinery, 6 weekends, honest hours count." / "The build
  I'd do with only $400 of tools."

**Pillar 3 — Pro Habits for Hobbyists (NURTURE/GROW)**
- Covers: the unglamorous commercial-shop habits — reference faces, story sticks,
  cut-list discipline — that separate clean work from lucky work.
- Serves: transferable in one video; makes viewers measurably better, which is what
  earns course trust.
- Ideas: "The one layout habit from my cabinet-shop years that fixes 80% of gaps." /
  "Story sticks: measure once, ever."

**Pillar 4 — Joinery Foundations: Course & Student Work (CONVERT)**
- Covers: lesson previews, student before/afters, honest launch updates.
- Serves: the launch. Student results are the proof; previews set the standard.
- Ideas: "A student's first hand-cut dovetail vs. their tenth (6 weeks apart)." /
  "One full lesson from the course, free: cutting to a knife line."

**Deliberate demotion — tool reviews.** They stay only as segments *inside* builds
("what I used and why"), never standalone. They're your best views but they attract
deal-hunters, not students — the waitlist data already shows it. Expect a short-term
view dip; the kit trades reach you can't monetise for an audience you can.
(draft — confirm you can live with the dip)

## 4. Voice & tone

- **Shop-floor plain.**
  Do: "Flatten one face first. Everything references off it."
  Don't: "Leverage a systematic approach to stock preparation."
- **Quietly exacting.**
  Do: "That gap is 0.5mm. It'll read as a shadow line from a metre away — your call."
  Don't: "Close enough! Nobody will notice!"
- **Encouraging without coddling.**
  Do: "Your third dovetail will still be ugly. Cut it anyway — number ten won't be."
  Don't: "Amazing job!! You're crushing it!! 🔥🔥"
- **Words you use:** furniture-grade, reference face, sneak up on the fit, honest
  hours, callback (as in repair), "cut to the line, not near it."
- **Words you avoid:** hack, game-changer, quick and easy, insane/crazy, must-have,
  anything with more than one exclamation mark.
- **In-voice sample:** "You don't need a jointer — you need one flat face and the
  patience to make it. I'll show you the same trick I used for 19 years when the
  shop's jointer was down and the deadline wasn't."

## 5. Bio & handles

- **Profile bio (127 chars):** "Ex-commercial cabinetmaker teaching weekend
  woodworkers furniture-grade joinery — in a one-car garage, without $10K of machines."
- **Longer about-line:** "19 years building cabinetry for a living; now I teach the
  joinery half of that trade to people with day jobs and garage shops. No gear hype —
  the course, the builds, and the habits that make joints last. *Joinery Foundations*
  opens October."
- **Handles:** consolidate to **@CallahanWoodshop** everywhere. Retire
  @rheabuildsstuff on Instagram (draft — confirm; you'll keep the account and pin a
  redirect post for 90 days so search traffic follows).

## 6. Reuse note

Paste this kit into the "🧠 Your context" box in the Playground, or save as
`CONTEXT.md` next to your drafts. Then [[content-repurposer]] atomizes each build
video in this voice, [[hook-writer]] writes hooks that sound shop-floor plain instead
of generic, [[short-form-script]] scripts Reels for Pillar 1, and
[[newsletter-writer]] drafts the launch sequence for Pillar 4. When any output drifts
hype-ward, the do/don't pairs in §4 are the correction reference.
```

## Why it's shaped this way

- **The niche is three-part specific, not a category** — "weekend woodworkers + furniture-grade joinery + hand-tool-first in a garage" follows the skill's audience + topic + angle formula, because the anti-patterns ban "a vague niche ('lifestyle', 'tech') that positions against everyone" — and "hobbyists, I guess" was exactly that.
- **Unconfirmed calls carry a *(draft — confirm)* label instead of a question** — the skill says given a rough brief, "build the full kit anyway" and label choices; so the tool-review demotion and the handle retirement are decided, flagged, and reversible rather than left as open questions back to Rhea.
- **The four pillars are labelled GROW / NURTURE / CONVERT** — the format demands "a mix of *grow* (reach), *nurture* (trust), and *convert* (sell)", and the launch goal means the convert pillar can't be an afterthought; each pillar states why it serves the audience plus 2–3 concrete post ideas, per the format.
- **Tool reviews are demoted in writing, with the expected cost stated** — the realistic tension in the brief (views vs. waitlist conversions) becomes an explicit trade in the kit, because pillars must be "durable themes", not whatever performs — and hiding the view-dip consequence would make the kit a sales pitch, not a plan.
- **Every voice attribute ships with a do/don't pair and there's a two-sentence in-voice sample** — the quality checks require voice "described with do/don't examples, not just adjectives"; "no-nonsense but not mean" from the brief is translated into three attributes another writer could actually imitate.
- **The bio says who it's for and what they get in 127 characters** — the anti-patterns reject "a clever bio that doesn't say who it helps"; the character count is stated so the platform-limit quality check is verifiable at a glance.
- **The reuse note names the specific downstream skills** — the kit's whole point is being "the 'reads-first' of the creator stack", so §6 wires it to [[content-repurposer]] and friends rather than ending at the bio like a static document.
