# Worked Example — Renewal Playbook

A gold-standard run of this skill: a renewal where the honest play is a downsell — and where saying so first is what protects the other $132K.

## The brief

Loomline — a fictional martech vendor selling marketing attribution, journey orchestration, and warehouse sync — has Fernwood Outdoor Co., a DTC outdoor-gear retailer, renewing 15 October 2026 at $180K ARR ($96K attribution, $60K orchestration, $24K warehouse sync). The attribution module is a genuine hit: it drove a reallocation of $1.4M in ad spend and cut blended CAC 18%. The orchestration module has sent zero journeys since February — Fernwood standardised on their email platform's built-in journeys instead. The CFO has ordered a company-wide software cost review, and the champion warns that "the unused module line item is exactly what they're hunting for." Today is 14 July: 93 days out, right at the 90-day wire.

## 6. Commercial Strategy

**Renewal scenario planning:**

| Scenario | Probability | ARR outcome | Response strategy |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Right-sized renewal** (recommended) | 55% | $132K (or $150K with tracking add-on) | Lead with it at W–10; frame as our proposal, not their concession |
| **Flat renewal** | 15% | $180K | Only if the orchestration activation miracle happens by September — do not bank on it |
| **Contraction beyond right-size** | 15% | $96K — attribution only | Defend warehouse sync with the activation-plan results; concede only if sync adoption hasn't moved by W–4 |
| **Churn risk** | 15% | $0 | Triggered if email-platform bundle lands before we've right-sized; escalate to VP CS + exec sponsor call |

**Discount guardrails:**
- Floor discount: 8% on the right-sized base — below that requires VP approval; the downsell *is* the concession, so cash discount on top must be exceptional
- Triggers for discount: 24-month term, or signed case study on the CAC result
- What to ask for in return: co-signed CAC case study; CMO as a reference call; named orchestration add-back trigger written into the order form

**Pricing flexibility:**
- Can hold 2026 unit pricing on the add-back clause (orchestration returns at $60K, not repriced) in exchange for the 24-month term
- Can phase server-side tracking at $9K for 6 months, then $18K, to fit this fiscal year's frozen budget

## 7. Objection Responses

**"We've been paying $60K a year for a module we don't use"**
> Do not defend it — it's true, and treating it as a haggle would confirm the CFO's suspicion. "You're right, and that's why our proposal already removes it. Here's the contract at $132K for what your team uses every day — and an add-back price locked in if journeys come back to Loomline." This objection is genuine dissatisfaction, not a tactic; resolution comes before negotiation.

**"Our email platform says it can do attribution too — why keep both?"**
> Ask before answering: "Which decisions would you run on it — channel budget reallocation, or campaign-level reporting?" Then map honestly: bundled attribution-lite covers last-touch reporting; Fernwood's $1.4M reallocation ran on multi-touch modelling the bundle doesn't do. Offer a side-by-side on their own Q2 data rather than a slideware comparison.

**"The CFO wants 20% off everything in the review"**
> Separate the two moves: "We've already taken $48K out of this line by removing what you don't use — that's a 27% reduction, self-imposed. A further discount on the working product needs something in return: the 24-month term takes the base to 8% below list." Do not stack a panic discount on top of a structural downsell.

**"Can we go month-to-month while the cost review finishes?"**
> Understand the driver first — if it's genuine uncertainty, offer a 6-month bridge at current per-month pricing with the annual right-sized offer held open; if it's procurement leverage, hold the annual proposal and its locked add-back clause as the better deal it genuinely is.

## Why it's shaped this way

- **The target ARR is a downsell, stated in the header, not buried** — the anti-pattern says "do not build a renewal strategy without first honestly assessing account health — wishful thinking leads to last-minute churn"; a flat $180K target would be exactly that wishful thinking, and the playbook prices honesty at $48K to protect $132K.
- **The shelfware objection is treated as genuine dissatisfaction, not a haggle** — per the anti-pattern "do not treat all renewal objections as negotiating tactics"; the response concedes the module before negotiating anything else, which also defuses the internal detractor (Risk 3).
- **No discount is offered as the first move** — the anti-pattern requires exploring value gaps before reducing price; the structural right-size *is* the value-gap fix, the 8% floor and VP gate satisfy the quality check for "a floor discount and a reason-to-discount framework," and every concession names an ask in return.
- **The economic buyer is mapped even though we've never met her** — the quality check demands the stakeholder map "includes the economic buyer — not just the champion"; the multi-thread plan routes to the CFO *through* armed intermediaries rather than pretending the cold relationship doesn't matter.
- **Both H/H risks carry pre-emptive mitigations with dates** — per the quality check "risk register has a mitigation for every H/H risk"; the core move (present the downsell ourselves at W–10) appears in the risk register, the commercial strategy, and the timeline, so the plan is one coherent play, not three documents.
- **The timeline starts immediately and says why it's compressed** — the anti-pattern bars starting under 90 days for accounts over $50K ARR; at 93 days this playbook is legal but at the wire, and the W–13 note makes the compression explicit instead of quietly renumbering the template.
- **Even a downsell renewal contains an expansion motion** — per the anti-pattern "every renewal is also an expansion conversation": the server-side tracking add-on, the priced add-back clause, and the 24-month term option all ride on the right-sized base, sequenced *after* trust is re-established.
- **Scenario probabilities sum to 100% and the numbers reconcile** — 55+15+15+15 = 100, and every ARR figure decomposes into the actual module prices ($96K + $60K + $24K = $180K; $180K − $60K = $132K), so the commercial story survives a CFO's spreadsheet.
