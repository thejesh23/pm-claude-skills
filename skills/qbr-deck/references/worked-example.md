# Worked Example — QBR Deck

A gold-standard run of this skill: a QBR that grades its own missed goal honestly, hands the disputed metric to the customer instead of defending it, and still walks out with commitments on both sides.

## The brief

Sundial — a fictional HR-tech platform for frontline shift-worker engagement — runs its Q2 2026 QBR with Alder Hotels Group, a UK hospitality operator with 19 sites and 4,200 frontline employees. £96K ARR, customer since September 2024, renewal March 2027, 3,800 licensed seats. The quarter is mixed: frontline activation beat its goal, but manager check-in adoption missed badly (38% vs a 60% goal), a May payslip-integration outage burned trust, and Alder's People Analytics lead disputes Sundial's headline claim that the platform cut absence 14% — a new rota policy launched the same quarter. CSM Rachel Adeyemi prepares the deck.

## Slide 3: Last Quarter — Goals We Set Together (5 min)

| Goal | Set in Q1 QBR | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Frontline activation ≥70% of licensed seats | 62% at the time; push via site-champion programme | ✅ Achieved — 77% |
| Manager check-in adoption ≥60% of line managers | Roll out to all 19 sites with GM sponsorship | ❌ Missed — 38% |
| Pulse surveys live in all 19 UK sites | 9 sites live at Q1 | ⚠️ Partial — 14 of 19 |

**On the miss:** check-in adoption stalled because the workflow assumes managers work at a desk; Alder's line managers run floors with a phone in a pocket. Average check-in takes 11 minutes in the current UI. What changes: mobile quick-check-in ships in Q3 (Slide 7) and we re-baseline the goal at 55% by end of Q3 rather than pretending 60% is still on the table for the old workflow.

*Talking point: "Let's start with accountability. Two of three went well. The manager number is on us — the workflow doesn't fit how your managers actually work, and I'd rather redesign it than re-launch training on a broken flow."*

**Highlights:**
- Site-champion programme drove activation past goal — the 5 champion-led sites average 88% activation
- Pulse participation at 66% is the strongest engagement signal in the account

**Opportunity:**
- Check-in adoption is *falling*, not just short — managers who tried it in Q1 are quietly abandoning it. This is the metric to fix before renewal-year conversations, because manager-visible value is what your GMs will judge us on.

*Talking point: "Usage is up everywhere except the one place we most need it. I don't want to talk past that — Tom, you see this from the floor; what are your managers saying?"*

## Slide 5: Business Impact — Value Delivered (10 min)

Lead with outcomes, not activity.

**Outcome 1: Unplanned absence rate**
- Before: 6.8% (Q1 2026 average across 19 sites)
- Now: 5.85% (Q2 2026)
- Impact: a 14% relative reduction — roughly 1,150 recovered shift-hours per month at Alder's average shift length
- **Attribution is contested and we're saying so:** Alder introduced a new rota-fairness policy in April, the same quarter. The 5 pulse-active sites improved 1.4pts vs 0.6pts at the rest — which suggests a real platform effect *inside* a policy effect — but this deck does not claim all 14%.

**Outcome 2: Open-shift fill time**
- Before: 31 hours average to fill an open shift (Q1)
- Now: 19 hours (Q2)
- Impact: fewer agency call-outs; Alder's own finance estimate puts agency spend down £22K this quarter

**Outcome 3: New-starter 90-day attrition**
- Before: 34% (2025 baseline)
- Now: 28% among starters onboarded with Sundial check-ins
- Impact: ~9 fewer replacement hires per quarter at Alder's stated £2,400 cost-per-hire ≈ £21.6K/quarter

**Customer evidence:**
> "The Friday pulse is the first time head office hears about a problem before the weekend rota collapses." — Site GM, Manchester (Q2 pulse verbatim)

*Talking point: "Sarah — outcome 1 is the one I want to settle with you, not around you. Proposal: we jointly design a comparison across rota-policy-only sites vs policy-plus-pulse sites and let that number, whatever it is, be the one we both use going forward."*

## Why it's shaped this way

- **The missed goal is graded ❌ on slide 3, with a cause and a changed plan** — the Output Format demands "for any partial or missed goal: state what happened and what changes next quarter," and the anti-patterns warn that "suppressing problems erodes trust and misses renewal risks"; re-baselining to 55% is more credible than re-promising 60%.
- **The disputed absence metric is surfaced by the vendor, not defended** — the QBR principle "every metric should connect to a business result the customer cares about" cuts both ways: a number the customer's analyst rejects connects to nothing. The deck claims only the pulse-site differential and proposes a *joint* analysis, which converts a credibility fight into a commitment (slide 9, item 1).
- **The value slide leads with absence, fill time, and attrition — not logins** — per the anti-pattern "do not fill the QBR with product activity metrics"; usage numbers exist, but they live on slide 4 as context, and every slide-5 outcome is priced in Alder's own units (£22K agency spend, £2,400 cost-per-hire).
- **The customer holds well over 20 minutes of airtime** — slide 8 is a pure 10-minute facilitation block with prompt questions and an empty capture table, slide 5's talking point hands the floor to Sarah, and slide 4's hands it to Tom — satisfying the quality check and the anti-pattern against one-sided presentations.
- **Every roadmap row carries a "why it matters to Alder" tied to a stated goal or dispute** — the skill instructs "do not dump the full roadmap"; three items, one of which exists specifically to answer the attribution argument.
- **Mutual commitments have named owners and dates on both sides** — the quality check requires "real owners on both sides"; Alder owns two commitments (champions, rota dates), which is what makes the attribution analysis and the pulse rollout the customer's project too, not vendor homework.
- **The P1 outage gets its own row and a process change** — the support slide doesn't bury the payday incident under a falling-ticket trend; naming it alongside "what we're doing differently" is the trust-preserving move the anti-patterns exist to protect.
- **Every slide ends in a talking point, not a title** — per the quality checklist; the slide-1 talking point even pre-announces the two uncomfortable topics, which sets the "conversation, not presentation" contract from minute one.
