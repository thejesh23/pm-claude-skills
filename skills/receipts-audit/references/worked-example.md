# Worked Example — Receipts Audit

## The brief

A startup's landing page draft claims: "Trusted by 200+ teams. Cuts reporting time by 80%. SOC 2 certified. The #1-rated analytics tool on G2." Sources provided: a customer list (147 paying + 61 free-tier), one case study (a customer went from 10 hrs → 2.5 hrs weekly), a SOC 2 **Type I** report dated 14 months ago, and a G2 screenshot: 4.7 stars, category rank #3.

## The output

# Receipts Audit: landing-page draft vs provided sources

## Claim Ledger

| # | Claim | Source offered | Grade | The gap |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | "Trusted by 200+ teams" | Customer list: 147 paying, 61 free | **Partially evidenced** | 208 accounts exist; "trusted by" implies paying use. 147 is the defensible number — or "used by 200+" if free tiers count |
| 2 | "Cuts reporting time by 80%" | One case study: 10 → 2.5 hrs (75%) | **Unsupported as stated** | n=1, and the arithmetic is 75%, not 80%. A single customer's result stated as a product property is the ledger's biggest liability |
| 3 | "SOC 2 certified" | Type I report, 14 months old | **Contradicted in nuance** | Type I attests design at a point in time — 14 months ago. "Certified" implies current Type II. Security buyers check; this claim invites the exact scrutiny it's meant to satisfy |
| 4 | "#1-rated analytics tool on G2" | Screenshot: 4.7★, rank #3 | **Contradicted** | The source disproves the claim. #3 with 4.7 stars is genuinely good — the lie is also unnecessary |

**Honesty score: 1 of 4 claims survives contact with its own evidence** (method: each claim graded solely against supplied sources; plausibility explicitly not credited).

## Ranked by load-bearing weight

1. **#3 (SOC 2)** — the only claim with legal/procurement consequences; it fails diligence questionnaires, not just vibes.
2. **#2 (80%)** — the conversion headline; also the easiest to fix honestly.
3. **#4 (#1-rated)** — disproven by its own screenshot; competitors notice these.
4. **#1 (200+ teams)** — nearest to true; smallest fix.

## Fix-or-drop

| # | Verdict | Replacement wording (bound to the source) |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Fix | "147 paying teams — and counting" *(the specific number reads MORE credible than the round one)* |
| 2 | Fix | "Meridian Freight cut weekly reporting from 10 hours to 2.5" — named, sourced, and stronger than the fake aggregate |
| 3 | Fix | "SOC 2 Type I attested (2025); Type II in progress" — and start the Type II |
| 4 | Fix | "Rated 4.7/5 on G2" — drop the rank or earn it |

## Why it's shaped this way

- **Every grade cites the gap between claim and source**, never plausibility — the 80% claim might even be true across customers, but the *provided evidence* says 75%, once; the audit grades receipts, not likelihood.
- **One contradiction outweighs the evidenced claims** — the scoring note says so explicitly; a page that's 75% fine and 25% disproven is a disproven page to a skeptical reader.
- **Load-bearing ranking puts SOC 2 first** despite #4 being "more wrong" — consequence-weighting, per the framework.
- **Every fix binds wording to a real source line** — the anti-patterns ban fixing-by-vaguening; note that three of four honest versions are *rhetorically stronger* than the inflated originals, which is the audit's quiet lesson.
