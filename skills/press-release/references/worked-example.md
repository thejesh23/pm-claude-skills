# Worked Example — Press Release Skill

## The brief

Suncrest Dynamics (fictional; Sacramento, 240 employees, 11,400 home battery systems installed across the US Southwest) is launching the Suncrest Arc on 14 July 2026: a 13.5 kWh home battery priced at $7,400 before installation and incentives, the first in its price class with vehicle-to-home (V2H) support — a parked EV can feed the home's essential circuits through the Arc. A new rail-mount design cuts median installation from 7.5 to 4.5 hours (40%). Pilot: 212 homes in Fresno and Tucson since November 2025. Availability at launch is California, Texas, and Arizona only, from 8 September. Marketing's draft copy claimed "true energy independence"; engineering pushed back that the product is an outage bridge, not an off-grid system. Target media: energy trade press and national consumer-tech desks. CEO Marisol Vega will give the quote.

# $7,400 home battery turns parked EVs into backup power
## The Suncrest Arc pairs 13.5 kWh of storage with vehicle-to-home charging and cuts installation time 40% — reservations open today, first installs in California, Texas and Arizona from 8 September

**SACRAMENTO, Calif., 14 July 2026** — Suncrest Dynamics today opened reservations for the Arc, a 13.5 kWh home battery that can draw additional backup power from an electric vehicle parked in the driveway — the first residential battery under $8,000 to support vehicle-to-home charging. The Arc costs $7,400 before installation and incentives, and installations begin 8 September in California, Texas and Arizona.

Home batteries in this capacity class typically carry a household's essential circuits — refrigeration, networking, well pumps, medical equipment — for around 18 hours. With a mid-size EV connected through the Arc's vehicle-to-home adapter, that extends to roughly three days. The Arc is designed as an outage bridge, not an off-grid system: across its three launch states, the median grid outage last year lasted under eight hours, and Suncrest says the product is built and priced for that reality rather than for full grid independence. A redesigned rail mount also cuts the median installation from 7.5 hours to 4.5 — labour that typically accounts for close to a third of a home battery's installed cost.

"Every battery ad shows a blacked-out street and one glowing house, and the honest version of this product is smaller than that," said Marisol Vega, CEO of Suncrest Dynamics. "When the grid fails, your food, your internet and your medical devices keep running — and the car you already own becomes three more days of that. We priced the Arc so a normal household doesn't have to finance it like a second car."

The Arc has been running in 212 pilot homes in Fresno, Calif. and Tucson, Ariz. since November 2025; 96% of pilot installations were completed in a single visit. Dana Whitfield, a retired nurse in Tucson and pilot participant, credits the system with carrying her through an 11-hour outage in March: "My oxygen concentrator never blinked. The next morning I ran the refrigerator off my daughter's EV while the crews were still working up the street."

Reservations open today at suncrestdynamics.example with a $100 refundable deposit. The vehicle-to-home adapter works with CCS-equipped EVs sold after 2022; a NACS version is planned for early 2027, alongside availability in eight additional states.

## Why it's shaped this way

- **The headline leads with the news, not the company** — "$7,400 home battery turns parked EVs into backup power" is nine words, active verb, price and capability included — per the Headline Rules (active voice, specific, under 10 words, never start with the company name). "Suncrest Dynamics announces revolutionary energy solution" would break all four at once.
- **The first paragraph passes the run-it-alone test**: who, what, price, the one genuinely new thing (V2H under $8,000), and when/where it ships. A journalist could publish that paragraph verbatim, which is the Quality Check — no founding story, no market-size throat-clearing.
- **The hype claim is visibly toned down rather than deleted.** Marketing's "true energy independence" became "an outage bridge, not an off-grid system," backed by the median-outage statistic. Releases that overclaim get fact-checked in the journalist's first reply; putting the limitation in the company's own mouth converts a vulnerability into a credibility signal — the anti-pattern here is promotional language, and the fix is candour with numbers.
- **The CEO quote says something the facts don't.** Vega critiques her own industry's advertising and explains the pricing philosophy — neither appears elsewhere in the release. A quote restating the headline is a named anti-pattern; this one would survive being the only thing a journalist excerpts.
- **The human angle is a named, permissioned pilot customer with a specific outage** (11 hours, oxygen concentrator, the daughter's EV) — the Journalist Test asks for a human angle, and medical-equipment backup is the concrete stake the spec sheet can't convey.
- **Limited availability is stated plainly in the subhead and body** (three states, 8 September) instead of being buried in a footnote — journalists will discover it anyway, and a release that hides it reads as advertising, not news.
- **The apparatus is complete and factual**: release line up top, ENDS marker, a boilerplate that is dates-and-numbers rather than adjectives, and a media contact with hours and timezone — per the Quality Checks that the embargo/release line, factual boilerplate, and contact must all be present for the release to be usable.
